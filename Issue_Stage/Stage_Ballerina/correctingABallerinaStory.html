Correcting a Ballerina's Story: The Truth Behind Makletzova v. Diaghileff

By Vanessa Banni-Viñas*

I-Introduction

In 1916, while performing in Boston, ballerina Xenia Makletzova 1 was fired for insubordination by producer Sergei Diaghileff.2 She sued, and a jury found her refusal to follow his orders reasonable. On appeal, the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court upheld the verdict.3 As a result, anyone reading the case comes away convinced that Makletzova was a victim and Diaghileff was a brute. But certain facts that failed to make it into the record, and thus the opinion, lead to a very different conclusion.

II-A troubled tour begins

In 1909, convinced that ballet had become stuffy and dull, Diaghileff founded "Les Ballets Russes" ("The Russian Ballets").4 By 1914, the company had become successful enough that Diaghileff began looking into the possibility of an American tour.5 When Addie Kahn (the wife of Otto Kahn, the chairman of the Metropolitan Opera's board of directors) threw her support behind the idea, a booking was quickly arranged.6

Unable to work in Russia due to the war, Diaghileff rented a home in Switzerland7 and from there instructed Serge Grigoriev, his right hand man (as well as the company's rehearsal director), to hire Tamara Karsavina for the tour.8 When she had to beg off because she was pregnant,9 Grigoriev wrote to Olga Spessivtzeva and offered her the role of prima ballerina.10 Although initially interested, Spessivtzeva was convinced by critic Akin Volinsky, who disapproved of Diaghileff's modern corps de ballet, to decline.11 With these rejections hanging over his head, Grigoriev went to Moscow and while there hired Makletzova.12

Born in 1892 in Saint Petersburg,13 Makletzova had been dancing in public exhibitions since she was eight years old.14 After graduating from the Bolshoi Ballet School in 1908,15 she joined the Bolshoi Ballet Company as a soloist.16 By the time Grigoriev met her, she was under contract to the Imperial Government Theatre in Moscow.17

Impressed with her experience, Grigoriev told Diaghileff that he had found a suitable replacement for Karsavin. Diaghileff, however, thought otherwise and told Grigoriev to "continue looking for someone better, but failing that engage Makletzova."18 When Grigoriev's further searches proved fruitless, Diaghileff hired Makletzova.

On September 18,1915, Makletzova and Diaghileff signed a formal contract in Saint Petersburg.19 Written in Russian and running 10 paragraphs in length, its terms can be briefly summarized as follows:

  1. Makletzova agreed to be the Les Ballets Russes's prima ballerina until May 1,1916. She also agreed to attend rehearsals in Switzerland and appear in two charity events.
  2. Makletzova agreed to perform the starring role in six ballets: L'Oiseau de Feu [The Firebird]; Pavillon d'Armide [Armide's House]; Les Sylphides [The Nymphs]; Carnival; Petrouchka·, and Dafins and Kloia.
  3. Makletzova agreed not to dance or do "anything in her profession, publically or for private persons, or moving picture films," without Diaghileff's consent.
  4. Diaghilev agreed to pay Makletzova 2,000 francs a month until the company reached the United States. Once in the United States, Diaghileff agreed to pay Makletzova 4,250 rubles a month.20 Diaghileff also agreed to pay Makletzova's "railroad fares [and] steamship ticket, first class, wherever she may go for purposes of performing" and provide her with a return ticket to Moscow.
  5. Makletzova agreed to forfeit 200 rubles for each performance she missed due to illness.
  6. Diaghileff agreed that if Makletzova was required to pay to get out of her existing contract with the Imperial Government Theatre, Diaghileff would reimburse her up to a maximum of 2,000 rubles. Diaghileff also agreed to give Makletzova an advance of 3,000 rubles before she left Saint Petersburg.
  7. Makletzova and Diaghileff agreed that if either of them breached the contract, the other would be entitled to 18,500 rubles. In addition, the non-breaching party would be released from the contract.
  8. Diaghileff agreed that Makletzova would not be required to appear in more than two performances per evening. Diaghileff also agreed that daytime performances were to be held only once a week, during which Makletzova would be required to dance only one ballet.
  9. Diaghileff and Makletzova agreed that the contract could be extended by mutual consent to September 1,1915.
  10. Makletzova agreed to forfeit 100 rubles if she failed to attend a daytime performance.21

Now that Diaghileff had his prima ballerina, he assembled the rest of the troupe and began rehearsals.22 In December 1915, the company gave two European charity performances.23 With the shows having garnered superb reviews,24 the company set sail from Bordeaux on January 1,1916 in high spirits.25

In New York, the company's first performance took place on Monday, January 17, at the Century Theatre.26 For months, Edward L. Bernays, the Met's press agent, had been whetting the public's appetite by filling countless magazines and newspapers with stories about the company's arrival,27 and throughout the country manufacturers had been churning out products based on the company's distinctive costumes.28 But this massive advertising campaign had raised expectations too high, especially because it had been built around Nijinsky and Karsavin, neither of whom was with the tour.29 As a result, the opening night reviews were brutal, with most of the criticism aimed squarely at Makletzova.30 As one observer succinctly put it, "the weakest point of 'The Firebird' was the bird herself."31

On the other hand, audiences could not get enough of Makletzova's fellow dancer Lydia Lopokova32 and openly wondered why she did not have a bigger role.33 As if this was not bad enough, Diaghileff found himself under constant attack by conservatives, who accused him of obscenity, especially when the company performed Schéhérazade (which many felt promoted miscegenation).34

On Saturday, January 29, the company gave its final New York performance and moved on to Boston.35 Although Diaghileff was hopeful that the tour would now go better, things actually got much worse.

III-A lawsuit is born

The company's first performance in Boston took place on Monday, January 31, at the Boston Opera House.36 Shortly before the curtain rose, Makletzova was asked to perform The Enchanted Princess instead of Carnival. Although she had done so in New York,37 she now refused unless she was paid an extra $150. The next day, Makletzova and Diaghileff had a heated conversation about the incident, during which Diaghileff insisted that Makletzova had orally agreed to change paragraph 2 of her contract to include The Enchanted Princess.38 Makletzova disagreed, insisting that the pair had merely discussed the possibility of an amendment.39

Makletzova's unwillingness to perform The Enchanted Princess was not the only sore point between the pair. For some time, Makletzova had been refusing to dance with Alexander Gavriloff, claiming that his inexperience made him an unsafe partner. With audiences still rejecting Makletzova (and clamoring for Lopokova), Diaghileff had finally had enough. On Wednesday, February 2, he fired her and gave her roles to Lopokova.40

Upon learning that she had been terminated, Makletzova tried to have the police arrest Diaghileff.41 When this attempt failed, she went to the Russian consulate, where she was similarly rebuffed.42 Undaunted, she hired a local attorney named Joseph Michelman,43 who promptly filed a breach-of-contract lawsuit against Diaghileff.44 In his countersuit, Diaghileff claimed that Makletzova's insubordination had left him with no choice but to replace her.

The case was assigned to Judge Frederic H. Chase of the Suffolk County Superior Court in Boston,45 who announced the trial would begin on April 11, 1916.46 On Wednesday, April 12, the beautiful, dark-skinned, well-dressed47 Makletzova took the stand and captivated the courtroom with her version of what had happened.48

At the conclusion of the evidence, Judge Chase instructed the jury to determine:

  1. if Diaghileff and Makletzova had amended paragraph 2 of their original contract to include The Enchanted Princess;and,
  2. whether Makletzova's refusal to dance with Gavriloff had been justified.49

Answering these questions "no" and "yes," the jury awarded Makletzova $4,500.50

IV-Rewriting History

The relationship between Makletzova and Diaghileff was destined to end in conflict. While Makletzova was an outspoken woman who loved being the center of attention and jealously guarded her prerogatives,51 Diaghileff had a well-earned reputation for being demanding and difficult.52

Although they had gotten along in Europe, they came to blows almost as soon as they arrived in the United States. Indeed, during one of the early New York performances, Makletzova, already in costume and five minutes from going on, threatened to walk out and relented only after a great deal of coaxing.53

It was while they were in New York that Makletzova and Diaghileff first discussed substituting The Enchanted Princess for Carnival,54 When Makletzova agreed to perform The Enchanted Princess during the New York opening, Diaghileff thought that they had reached an agreement for the entire tour. As a result, he scheduled her to perform it again at the Boston opening.55

Makletzova later claimed she did not know she would be performing The Enchanted Princess until the morning of the Boston opening. Even if true,56 it is interesting that she waited until 6:30 p.m. to discuss the matter with Grigoriev.57 Moreover, at 9:00 p.m., just 10 minutes before The Enchanted Princess was scheduled to begin, Makletzova told Randolfo Barocchi, the Met's financial manager, that unless he immediately brought $150 to her dressing room she would not go on.58 Of course, Makletzova knew that with a full house and no one to replace her, Barocchi would have to comply.

Makletzova also testified that she was reluctant to perform The Enchanted Princess in Boston because she had injured her toes (an explanation she was forced to back away from during cross-examination).59 In fact, after dancing The Enchanted Princess at the New York opening, Makletzova had consulted Victor E. Gartz, an attorney whose clients included members of the Russian royal family.60 He advised her that her recent actions had not vitiated the contract, and that she was therefore free to turn down future requests to dance The Enchanted Princess.61 Of course, in front of the jury Makletzova sought to downplay the importance of her visit to Gartz (as well as gain sympathy) by claiming she was injured.

Makletzova's testimony regarding her refusal to perform with Gavriloff was even more outrageous:

  1. Makletzova claimed that Gavriloff was unsuitable because he was an ensemble member rather than a featured player,62 but later admitted he had performed The Enchanted Princess with Lopokova and others.63 In addition, Gavriloff, who had trained at the Maryinsky Ballet and joined the Les Ballets Russes in 1911,64 was considered by Diaghileff to be talented enough to serve as one of Nijinsky's understudies.65
  2. Makletzova said she was afraid she would be injured if she had to dance with Gavriloff, but let Diaghileff know she would be willing to do so if he paid her extra.66 Indeed, throughout her time with the company Makletzova repeatedly badgered Diaghileff for money, which suggests she either had very expensive tastes or very large debts (or both).67
  3. Makletzova claimed that she was asked to dance with Diaghileff without being given time to rehearse with him. In fact, however, she had repeatedly turned down his practice requests.68

V-Conclusion

With the benefit of hindsight, it is obvious that the jury reached the wrong verdict. On stage, Xenia Makletzova was merely an average dancer. In court, however, she was an undeniable star.
