Making Theatre Art

David Levine 's Habit

Sarah Kozinn

Director and performance-based visual artist David Levine opened his 2006 essay " Bad Art and Objecthood" (a response to Michael Fried 's 1967 essay "Art and Objecthood ") with this quote from Chris Burden: "It seems that bad art is theatre...Getting shot is for real... there's no element of pretense or make-believe in it" (in Levine 2006:22).1 In his 2012 production, Habit, Levine continued his engagement with Burden's provocative assertion that "bad art is theatre" but this time with a full-scale production of Jason Grote's 90-minute play, The Children of Kings, staged inside a replica of a suburban ranch house built within New York City's Essex Street Market. The performance was part of the French Institute Alliance Française's French Institute Alliance FrançaiseCrossing the Line Festival (coproduced with PS122 and funded in part by a Kickstarter campaign), and it was free and open to the public. No tickets were required. Two rotating casts of three characters performed the play on alternating days, nonstop, eight hours a day for ten days straight. For the performance's duration, the actors never left the set, which meant they had to take care of their emotional and biological needs while also attending to the circumstances of the written drama.

With this production, as in his 2006 essay, Levine seems to be challenging the same notion of theatre that compelled Burden to make his famous and provocative 1971 performance piece, Shoot, in which Burden's assistant shot him in the left arm. Theatre is pretense. Getting shot is real. But to limit the questions Habit poses to how events framed as theatre can simultaneously be real and authentic would be too reductive, and, more importantly, it would overlook the range of additional questions David Levine puts into play, such as: Is this a work of art about theatre, about experimenting and violating its rituals? Or is this theatre grappling with its objectness, its refusal of the conditions that make theatre, as Michael Fried wrote, "bad art"?

Habits of Theatre-Making

Levine first conceived Habit in 2005 as he began his move away from theatre and into visual arts performance. The questions Habit asks seem to come from Levine's desire to complicate habits of theatre-making by experimenting with form and ritual. He explores the essence of theatre by removing the signs that help define it as such by, for example, enclosing actors within a plywood box ('Night, Motherfucker [2004])2 or, as in Habit, by staging the performance in a museum, gallery, or warehouse and not in a conventional theatre space (Habit was developed at Robert Wilson 's Watermill Center in 2010 and at MASS MoCA in 2011). This removing and relocating asks us to consider if what is left is indeed still theatre. In Habit , David Levine thinks through "habits of realism, or what our habitual mode of realism is, or American theatre's habit of producing the same play over and over again" (in Katz 2012). He commissioned downtown playwright Jason Grote (who was also a television writer for the first season of Smash ) to compose a script that employed standard tropes and formulas of contemporary American realism exemplified in the works of playwrights such as Adam Rapp, Neil LaBute, and Sam Shepard: a suburban setting, a small cast of working-class characters, drug/ alcohol abuse, and a secret that when revealed has catastrophic consequences. Grote's play, initially named Half-Moon Mirror, a title that David Levine says recalls in style, theme, and title Adam Rapp's play Red Light Winter, and then changed to The Children of Kings, is about two drug-addled brothers, Mitch and Doug, who harbor a dark secret that, until the end of the play, they hide from their roommate, Viv, a failed sociology grad student turned-stripper (Holzapfel 2012).

In addition to examining genre, Levine also experiments with acting as both a job and a craft. Prior to Habit, Levine composed a 2007 piece called Actors at Work in which he hired actors under Actors' Equity contracts to continue working at their day jobs in order to reframe these jobs as theatrical employment. In his 2007 Bauerntheater (Farmers' Theatre) project, Levine and actor David Barlow rehearsed Heiner Miiller's play Die Umsiedlerin (1961), but instead of performing the play, David Levine invited audiences to a field two hours outside of Berlin, in the East German region of Brandenburg, to watch David Barlow—who played the leading role, a communist farmer named "Flint"—tend potatoes using traditional methods taught to him by Eckhardt Schlestein, an actual potato farmer. When describing this performance in a 2008 TDR article, Marvin Carlson invoked Michael Kirby's theories of matrixed and nonmatrixed performance. He wrote that Levine's intention with the piece short-circuited "the distinction between the real-life action of the Happening and the matrixed action of the theatre," an objective that is also at play in Habit (2008:36).

Levine complicates the ways the audience reads the signs (the matrixes) that help viewers determine what is or is not theatre. In Habit, Levine worked with two rotating casts of three actors who performed for the duration of the performance (eight hours) within the set and without ever leaving. During that time the actors had to negotiate their own needs with those of the characters. As David Levine put it, "I'm more interested in the job of being in character and the solutions you find to that" (in Katz 2012). This postulation draws attention to the conciliations actors are always making between their own needs or wants (hunger, sleepiness, the desire to impress a cast mate or an audience member...etc.) with the performance's needs. Levine's set-up made these negotiations all the more central, because in addition to their emotional needs/wants, the actors also had to contend with their biological ones. Levine described this layering of the actors' experiences with those of the characters — also a tenet of Lee Strasberg's acting method — as "not so much rejecting realism as wanting to provide actors with a platform to rock the fuck out on a style" (in Holzapfel 2012).

My Habits

I went to Habit on Tuesday afternoon September 25th at around 3:30 p.m. I read on the Crossing the Line Festival's website that the performance ran from 1:00 p.m. to 9:00 p.m., and I knew from the description that the actors would be performing the same text over and over during the allotted time: "Within a four walled, fully furnished and functional American ranch house (stocked refrigerator, working stove, plumbing, running water), three actors perform a 90-minute drama on a continuous loop for eight hours a day" (FIAF 2012). When I arrived, the door to the Essex Street Market Building B was open. The only indication that a performance was going on was a small sign advertising the festival standing on the side walk outside the entrance. Just inside a woman was stationed at an information table covered with pamphlets detailing the other festival performances, and just beyond her, in the center of the cavernous industrial space, was the set.

From the outside, the set looked like a construction site, and I had the feeling of being inside a television studio. The only parts of television studio sets that are decorated are the ones that appear on camera, so the exteriors and backsides of the walls are left raw and unfinished. The same went for Photo by Marsha Ginsberg's design. Only the inside of the set resembled a home, so the incoming audience was first confronted by the rough exterior, a persistent reminder that the barriers separating the actors' world and the audience's were built by designers for this performance event, not by a construction crew building a home. Photo by Ginsberg's design immediately helped frame the events inside as theatrical.

To see inside, visitors gathered by the windows like moths towards light. The interior of the four-walled structure was the actors' domain, and the space outside of it, the audience's. The actors never left their quarters, and from what I observed, the audiences always stayed outside in theirs. The area surrounding the set was a between-zone (between the street outside and the playing space) where visitors could talk to each other, make phone calls, eat, sit on a bench, read, tend to their children, peer in through windows—whatever they wanted to do. This space was unstructured, open, and unchoreographed. Inside, the actors spoke Grote's text, but they were free to wander from room to room, eat, cook, piss, shower, shit, nap — as long as these actions also worked to advance the plot. While there was action going on outside the set, the kind of action akin to the behavior of museumgoers — meandering, chatting, texting, children crying—the dramatic action took place within it. The structure's central location, like the nucleus of a cell, demarcated the playing space as the center of this performance universe. Concurrently, the area surrounding the set was like the margin on a piece of lined paper, and the audience was the doodles and jottings that were not critical to the content, but evidenced the wandering thoughts, the needed time-outs, and the creative explorations that existed simultaneously— sometimes distracting from the content and sometimes complementing it.

The staging also brought to mind another analogy: While peering through the kitchen window at the character Viv giving Mitch a lap dance, I had the memory of standing in front of the Bonobo monkey habitat at the San Diego Zoo. Visitors wandered at will up to the window to watch the animals in captivity have sex, eat bananas, climb trees, fight, and go to the bathroom all while caged inside a space designed to resemble their natural habitat, the lowland rainforest in the Democratic Republic of Congo. Photo by Ginsberg's set was a kind of habitat for realism's stock characters (two sparring, drug addicted brothers harboring a secret and the young, sexually charged woman who pits the brothers against each other), and we, the audience/visitors/tourists/curious wanderers were free to study these characters at no charge. During that time, the actors performed Grote's text and went through their routines, whether or not anyone was watching—just like animals at the zoo.

This design produced a greater-than normal distance between actors and audience, even though, on its face, the concept seemed akin to the kind of immersive theatre experiences that have recently become fashionable in New York City such as Punchdrunk's Sleep No More (2011-ongoing), Dave Malloy's Natasha, Pierre & The Great Comet of 1812 (2012-2014), and Third Rail Projects' Then She Fell (2012- ongoing). However, while these shows bring the audiences into the performance, Habit excluded the audience from it. The way the actors seemed unaffected by the audience, even when they were only inches from their faces, and the way they roamed from room to room and sometimes to areas beyond the audience's sight lines as if it did not matter what the audience could or could not see, magnified the separation between audience and performer—and not just geographically, but also psychologically. Being ignored and physically blocked from them was alienating, and, in my experience, the staging compromised the give and take so essential to the theatrical experience. Jerzy Grotowski wrote that theatre can exist with out makeup, costumes, stage lights, and sets, but the one thing it cannot "live" without is "the actor-spectator relationship of perceptual, direct, 'live' communion" ([1968] 2012:19). The absence of this "communion" felt very much a part of Levine's experiment in non-theatre theatre. Levine described this as the anti-the atrical part of the performance: "I finally man aged to create this anti-Fried piece of theatre, which actually doesn't need you to complete it because it'll do its own thing no matter what [...] It's like, 'Oh yeah, it'll run...it's a machine... it turns, we don't have to be there...It'll just keep going in there all day'" (in Holzapfel 2012:107). So if, as Fried believed, bad art is theatre because it panders to and depends on an audience, then Levine resists this interpretation by trying to make a performance that goes on (and on) with or without one.

Habits of Viewing

When I arrived at Habit, the actors had already completed a quarter of the script. Of course, I would not discover this until the play came to its end. I knew it was the end because all the characters had been shot—but not like when Chris Burden was shot. The actors had succumbed to the pretense of being shot, to a fake gun jerked up and a loud bang, and were hunched over themselves on the bedroom floor, on the bathroom floor, and on the bed. They stayed like this for several minutes until Mitch "woke up," the first action at the beginning of the play, as if nothing had happened, and began the run again. After about 15 minutes the play felt familiar, and I knew the actors had reached the same place in the text as when I arrived. At this point the experience shifted to one layered with my memory of that first watching, and I stayed a few more minutes before taking a break on one of the benches.

Though the audience had to move around the set in order to follow the action, Habit was less about the exchange of space, and more about the exchange of attention from the actors' actions to the audiences'. In the same way one can only watch the Bonobo monkeys for so long before staring instead at the reflection of the other visitors watching the monkeys, during Habit my interest wandered from the inside of the set to a young man resting against the kitchen door entrance. He looked exhausted, and he struggled to keep his eyes open. They would close, and then he would jerk awake, then his eyes would droop, his body would sway, his eyes would close, and he would jerk awake, and so on and so forth. Framed by the doorway, this young man's performance marked a stark contrast to the actors' behavior.

While the actors performed snorting cocaine, sleeping, and dying this young man was actually falling asleep, teetering forward and back, threatening to fall over into the set. I never once felt concern for the actors, even as they pointed a gun at each other, but as the young man swayed forward I felt the urge to rush across the warehouse to keep him upright. David Levine's project created an environment in which audiences became voyeurs who watched actors, confined within a set, coping with performing the same drama over and over again for eight hours. However, this configuration made the performers' actions feel more hyperreal than real—always mediated through the lens of "this is for show." Intended or not, the performance succumbed to what I call the "Reality TV effect," a skeptical frame of reference through which viewers consider the "authentic" as staged, much like reality TV—scripted, but not totally. (It is quite apropos that on the Kickstarter page the show was billed as "The Real World meets No Exit" [Kickstarter 2012].) So when looking for performances with consequence, performances that could threaten or disrupt the flow of the piece because of their inescapable realness, I located the young man against the doorframe who might have, at any point, tumbled into the house. He was not one of David Levine's hired actors, but in that moment he was as much a part of Habit as they were. The conditions of the performance invited this analysis, so I feel at liberty to discuss the slumbering man's per formance alongside those of Mitch, Viv, and Doug. I would like to think that this impulse to study other audience members as one would the characters is part of Habit's challenge. In other words, the piece makes us consider our habits of viewing and spectatorship. It also con fronts our habits of perception—how we perceive and interpret something to be or not to be theatre.

Habits of Perceiving

How do I understand my experience of Habit? In many ways, the signs point to theatre: The performance had a script, a creative staff, hired actors, publicity, a set, a time frame, and a glowing Ben Brantley review in the New York Times theatre section. However, Habit was void of many elements I love to experience when attending a theatre event: the live communion between actors and audiences and its transportive potential. Habit was cold theatre, cut off from the heat generated when people share a space in order to do theatre together. In "Dramaturgy of the Spectator", Marco De Marinis wrote: "Through its actions, by putting to work a range of definite semiotic strategies, the performance seeks to induce in each spectator a range of definite transformations, both intellectual (cognitive) and affective (ideas, beliefs, emotions, fantasies, values, etc.)" (1987:101). Watching Habit, I didn't feel like the objective of the director and performers was to do anything with me. This is not to say Habit was not affective; I certainly experienced a range of emotions and thoughts while at the performance. But the impact was almost in spite of the insularity of the performance. As such, I felt encouraged to back away, to stay outside the house and study the performance from a distance, much like I would a sculpture at a museum or gallery.

Perhaps this is why the pleasure I derived from Habit has grown in the days since seeing it. During this period of reflection I have not only enjoyed grappling with the conceptual questions Levine set in motion, but I have also been able to project myself inside the walls of Photo by Ginsberg's set, where I imaginatively experience the intimacy I sought while in the Essex Street Market. In other words, Levine's piece has made me consider the fundamental reasons why I love theatre—my own habits of enjoyment. So while Habit may have pushed against several of these by insisting on its self-sufficiency, an appreciation for these elements has been lit anew.
